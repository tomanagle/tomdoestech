## Context & Background

Solar designs are built from a finite set of hardware: panels, batteries, inverters, EV chargers. But the bill of materials (BOM) also needs a long tail of extra hardware and service items that depend on the site (roof type, slope, travel distance) and the design itself (number of panels, inverter type, and so on).

Encoding all of that in application code would have meant a constant maintenance burden for engineers and very little flexibility for the people who actually own the pricing and BOM logic. The Bill of Materials Automator is a rules engine that lets those users define rules which automatically add the right items to a BOM based on the design and site.

## Implementation

I worked with a product owner and another senior engineer to grow this from a single kind of rule into a rules engine that non-developers could safely author against. The people who own the pricing and BOM logic can define their own rules, and the engine applies them to a design to produce the bill of materials, rather than that logic being hard-coded by engineers.

A lot of the work went into the parts you don't see: the guardrails that keep operator-authored rules safe to run, and an execution model that applies rules in a predictable order so they compose together correctly.

## Outcomes

- Hundreds of rules authored in production.
- Thousands of customer quotes generated with accurate bills of materials.
- No production incidents to date.

## What Went Well

One of the rule types allowed the operator to write equations that return boolean values "should this SKU be added to the BOM?" and values "how many of this SKU should be added?". The engine was a simple interpreter that took in context from the solar design and the current BOM as the time of the execution. Because the syntax was strict and it only had 2 return types, writing the equations was simple enough for a non-developer, but made the engine much more flexible.

## What I'd Do Differently
The rules ended up being imperative: each rule type was a different way of telling the engine what to do, so an operator had to pick the right type before they could write anything, I'd make them declarative instead. A single rule format that holds all the building blocks (how components relate to each other and the conditions that apply) so operators just describe the rule they want and the engine works out how to run it. It would have been simpler to learn and simpler to extend, without adding a new rule type every time we needed something new.

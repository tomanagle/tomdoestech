## Context & Background

The platform emitted a high volume of domain events onto Kafka, but there was
no good way to analyse them. Product and business teams needed to query that
activity historically, and we needed somewhere to land it that wouldn't put
analytical load on the operational systems producing the events.

## Implementation

The first version was built entirely on AWS-native, mostly-serverless
components:

- **Kinesis Firehose** consumed the Kafka events and buffered them into
  batched objects in **S3**, which acted as the raw landing zone / data lake.
- **AWS Glue** ran the ETL — cataloguing the raw data and transforming it into
  a cleaned, structured form.
- The curated data was then queryable with **Athena**, giving analysts SQL over
  the data in S3 without standing up a warehouse.

<Callout type="info">
  The whole v1 pipeline was serverless and AWS-managed: Firehose for ingestion,
  S3 as the lake, Glue for ETL, Athena for query. Decoupling ingestion from
  processing meant the operational Kafka consumers were never on the critical
  path for analytics.
</Callout>

Later we re-platformed the transformation layer onto **dbt**, modelling the
data as version-controlled SQL, and loaded the transformed output into
**Snowflake** as the warehouse. dbt replaced the Glue ETL jobs and gave the
team a faster, more testable, and more maintainable transformation workflow.

## What Went Well

- Firehose → S3 cleanly decoupled ingestion from processing, so analytics work
  never affected the systems producing the events.
- Leaning on managed, serverless pieces (Firehose, Athena) kept the operational
  overhead low while we were proving the pipeline out.

## What I'd Do Differently

- The Glue-based ETL became hard to evolve and reason about as the
  transformations grew — which is ultimately why we moved to dbt and Snowflake.
  Knowing that, I'd reach for dbt + a warehouse earlier rather than hand-rolling
  ETL jobs in Glue.
